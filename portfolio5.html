Comparative Study on Different Types of Motherboards

The motherboard is the logic board, baseboard, system board, mainboard, main circuit board, planar board, and mobo in a short way. It is a nonconductive plastic sheet with the necessary circuit and placeholders like sockets/slots to connect components and provide logistics for all the components to work in a coordinated manner.


Thin narrow layers of Aluminum or Copper printed on the plastic sheet of the board act like a circuit that connects various components. It fits all the components in their designated places, making it a chassis.

Types of Motherboard

1. Micro ATX

Micro ATX boards are much smaller overall, but retain most of the functionality of full-sized ATX. It measures 9.6 inches x 9.6 inches (24.4 cm x 24.4 cm)


Micro ATX boards work well for home and office PC builds that don’t require much expandability but need to support moderate specifications at low cost.

2. Mini ITX Motherboard

The Mini ITX form factor defines the smallest possible motherboards available for desktop computers. These boards measure just 6.7 x 6.7 inches (17 x 17 cm).


Their tiny size and powerful functionality make Mini ITX boards perfect for compact, high-performance PCs, gaming systems, home theater PCs (HTPCs), and small form factor workstations. They work great when you need a lot of computing horsepower but minimal space.

3. ATX Motherboard

The ATX (Advanced Technology Extended) types of motherboard is the most popular desktop motherboard form factor today. These boards measure 12 x 9.6 inches (30.5 x 24.4 cm).


ATX boards power everything from essential home and office machines that run browsers and productivity software, to high-end gaming PCs and media workstations meant for computationally intensive work like video editing.

4. BTX Motherboard

Balanced Technology Extended or BTX was a relatively short-lived motherboard format introduced by Intel in 2004 to succeed the aging ATX design. BTX boards never gained much traction and the standard was discontinued after a few years. These days BTX boards are quite rare to find.

5. AT Motherboard

The AT form factor, short for Advanced Technology, preceded ATX and was very popular in the late 80s to mid-90s.


They had a large footprint and exposed peripheral ports at the back, leading to wiring issues. They were replaced by ATX which overcame some of these problems.

6. Pico BTX

Pico BTX or pBTX conforms to an ultra-compact motherboard standard measuring just 5.921 x 5.906 inches (151 x 150 mm).


They work well for custom embedded applications but high thermal output can be an issue given their extremely cramped PCB area and miniaturized components.

7. LPX Motherboard

LPX or Low Profile Extended defines motherboards made specifically for slim form factor PCs with tight vertical space constraints.


These motherboards have a narrow width and low profile expansion slots to take up less vertical room inside compact enclosures.

8. Extended ATX

Extended ATX is types of motherboard built even larger than standard AT sizes offer more real estate area to pack additional features and connectivity.


For example, there exist extended ATX boards sporting up to 10 PCIe slots for installing multiple graphics cards for hardcore gaming or GPUbased machine-learning workloads.

9. Flex ATX Motherboard

As revealed by its name, one unique aspect of the Flex ATX or FTX form factor is that the layout on the PCB has a certain flexibility, subject to keeping overall board dimensions within spec. This allows manufacturers some wiggle room with component placement.

10. Mini STX Motherboard

Mini STX is the newest types of motherboard form factor welcomed in the constantly evolving world of computer motherboard standards.


The mini STX platform utilizes standardized interposer cards that can flexibly extend I/O options by adding USB, Ethernet, WiFi, Thunderbolt, extra SATA ports and more.

11. Nano ATX

First introduced by ASRock in 2018, Nano ATX is a types of motherboard offer a design that’s sized in between mini ITX and micro ATX boards. Nano ATX conforms to the 12 x 8.3 inch (30.5 x 21 cm) form factor.


The main goal of the Nano ATX standard is to provide additional motherboard options with a smaller PCB area than micro ATX, while retaining support for full-sized add-on cards that mini ITX can’t fit.

12. NLX Motherboard

LX motherboards are just 1.2 inches or 30mm in height compared to the roughly 1.8 inches or 45mm of standard LPX boards. This means only specially designed low-profile graphics cards and peripherals can be used with NLX systems.


The highly niche format suits a small segment of specialty computing devices that demand tiny volumes while maximizing space utilization inside homes, offices and data centers.
