Computer Science as Discipline, Computing Disciplines and Majors

History of Computer Science

  • 1930s-40s: The rise of mathematics and the first digital computer
  • 1950s-60s: The birth of computer science and advancements in hardware
  • 1970s and 80s: Trends in databases, personal computing, and the early internet
  • 1990s and 2000s: Emergence of PCs and scientific supercomputers, along with the birth of the cloud
  • 2010s onwards: Advancements in programming languages and cloud leading up to AI

Development of Computer Science

  • Computer science emerged as an independent discipline in the early 1960s. The roots of computer science lie primarily in the related fields of mathematics, electrical engineering, physics, and management information systems.
  • Mathematics is the source of two key concepts in the development of the computer—the idea that all information can be represented as sequences of zeros and ones and the abstract notion of a “stored program”.
  • The relative ease with which two states (e.g., high and low voltage) can be realized in electrical and electronic devices led naturally to the binary digit, or bit, becoming the basic unit of data storage and transmission in a computer system.
  • Electrical engineering provides the basics of circuit design—namely, the idea that electrical impulses input to a circuit can be combined using Boolean algebra to produce arbitrary outputs. The invention of the transistor and the miniaturization of circuits, along with the invention of electronic, magnetic, and optical media for the storage and transmission of information, resulted from advances in electrical engineering and physics.
  • Management information systems, originally called data processing systems, provided early ideas from which various computer science concepts such as sorting, searching, databases, information retrieval, and graphical user interfaces evolved.

Computer Science as Discipline

  • is the study of computers and computing as well as their theoretical and practical applications.
  • applies the principles of mathematics, engineering, and logic to a plethora of functions including:
    • algorithm formulation,
    • software and hardware development, and
    • artificial intelligence
  • as a result, the field of computer science focuses as much on the theoretical underpinnings of computers as it does their actual uses and creation.
  • is considered as part of a family of five separate yet interrelated disciplines:
    • computer science,
    • computer engineering,
    • information systems,
    • information technology, and
    • software engineering

Principal Subdivisions of the Field

-the subject matter of computing can be broadly divided into two parts: applications and systems


COMPUTER APPLICATIONS

- Studies information processing tasks and their related data representations

- Can be subdivided into numerical and nonnumerical categories

- Numerical applications are those in which mathematical models and numerical data are dominant

- Nonnumerical applications are those in which problems and information are represented as symbols and rules

COMPUTER SYSTEMS

- Studies structures, mechanisms, and schemes for processing information

- Can be subdivided into software systems and hardware systems

- Software systems are concerned with machine-level representations of programs and data, schemes for controlling program execution, compilers, programming environments, network communications and management, and operating systems

- Hardware systems are concerned with logical design, machine organization, processors, memory, and devices in various -5- technologies such as VLSI, silicon, and GaAs

- Computer architecture and computer engineering are concerned with both software and hardware

Five Computing Disciplines and Majors

  1. Computer Engineering
    • CE focuses on the design and construction of computers and computer-based systems/devices (i.e., hardware and related software). CE has a strong engineering orientation.
  2. Computer Science
    • CS views computing from a broad, scientific basis, focusing on: 1) developing effective ways to solve computing problems, 2) devising new ways to use computers, and 3) designing and implementing software
  3. Information Systems
    • IS specialists select and acquire, the software products developed by Computer Scientists, and the comprehensive applications developed by Software Engineers, deploy them on top of the infrastructure deployed by IT specialists, and integrate these products into a functional solution.
  4. Information Technology
    • Focuses on the management and processing of information within business and other organizations.
  5. Software Engineering
    • SE combines computer science principles and techniques with engineering practices to design and create comprehensive and large-scale “applications software”.
